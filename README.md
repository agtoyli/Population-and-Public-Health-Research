# Machine Learning for Prediction of Emerging Infectious Diseases (EID)

## Project Overview
In this project, I worked with collaborator [Daniel Middleton](https://github.com/damiddle) to perform a comprehensive literature review of the integration of machine learning and AI to predict, monitor, and respond to emerging infectious diseases (EID). As EIDs continue to pose severe threats to global health, this research examines how public health officials can utilize machine learning to identify outbreaks early and implement effective containment measures.

## Key Research Areas
### 1. Population and Public Health Impact
We identified several critical domains in which machine learning can improve public health outcomes:
- **Early Intervention:** Early identification and preventative action can limit spread of disease
- **Resource Allocation:** Predict which regions will benefit most from additional personnel and supplies
- **Risk Stratifications:** Identify high-risk populations who are in greatest need of protection

### 2. Technical Implementations 
We summarized diverse machine learning applications previous studies used for EID, including:
-**Digital Twins:** Create virtual representation of reality to simulate effects of containment measures
-**Natural Language Processing:** Monitor public sentiment through social media to adapt public health messaging
-**Transfer Learning:** Supplement limited information with learning about disease mechanisms and transmission from previous epidemics

### 3. Challenges for Future Improvement
Machine learing for EID prediction faces the same challenges as all AI implementations:
-**Data Quality:** Epidemics often emerge in countries with limited public health infrastructure, making data collection difficult
-**Model Generalizability:** Overfitting may occur with limited data, hampering performance in external populations or alternate strains
-**Infrastructure:** Training advanced models is resource-intensive

## Conclusion
Machine learning is a promising tool for rapid disease response, but its effectiveness remains tied to data quality. As AI infrastructure becomes more widespread, machine learning can greatly supplement public health officials' ability to manage EIDs, but continually re-evaluating model bias is critical.
